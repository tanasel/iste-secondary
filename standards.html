THE 7 STANDARDS

The 7 ISTE Standards for Students

Each standard below connects the framework to classroom practice: what it means, secondary examples, a “try this Monday” activity and evidence to look for.

Referenced from the ISTE Standards for Students © 2024 (v4.02), summarised and adapted for secondary-classroom use. Official text at iste.org/standards.

1.1
ISTE 1.1

Empowered Learner

Students set goals, seek feedback, and own how they learn.

What it means

Students leverage technology to take an active role in choosing, achieving and demonstrating competency in their learning goals, informed by the learning sciences. They build personal learning networks, customise their learning environment, use feedback to improve, and grow confident with current — and emerging — technologies.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Students set unit goals and track them in a digital portfolio (Google Sites/Docs).
  • Use self-marking quizzes for instant formative feedback and act on it.
  • Let students choose how to show mastery (screencast, doc, build).
  • Keep a reflection log after each assessment.

Try this Monday

Start a unit by asking each student to write one personal goal and one success criterion in a shared doc — revisit it at the midpoint.

Look-fors

  • Students can name what they're getting better at.
  • They use feedback to revise, not just receive it.
  • They troubleshoot common tool problems themselves.
  • They explore new and emerging tools thoughtfully.
1.2
ISTE 1.2

Digital Citizen

Students contribute positively, safely and ethically to their digital communities.

What it means

Students recognise the responsibilities and opportunities for positively contributing to their digital communities. They manage their digital footprint, interact with empathy and inclusion, safeguard their well-being and the time they spend online, and protect their privacy, data and security — making safe, legal and ethical decisions.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Audit your digital footprint and discuss its lasting impact.
  • Model empathetic, inclusive online interaction.
  • Reflect on well-being and intentional, balanced screen-time.
  • Protect privacy, data and security (passwords/2FA, app permissions).

Try this Monday

Have students search themselves (or a fictional persona) and map what a stranger could learn — then write one thing they'd change.

Look-fors

  • They can explain a privacy or data risk in their own words.
  • They interact with empathy and challenge unkind or unsafe behaviour.
  • They make intentional choices about their time and well-being online.
1.3
ISTE 1.3

Knowledge Constructor

Students research critically and build their own understanding.

What it means

Students critically curate a variety of resources using digital tools to construct knowledge, produce creative artefacts and make meaningful learning experiences for themselves and others. They evaluate the accuracy, validity, bias, origin and relevance of digital content — increasingly including AI-generated content.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Teach lateral reading and source triangulation.
  • Evaluate an AI answer for accuracy and bias before using it.
  • Build an annotated, curated resource set on a topic.
  • Investigate a real-world local issue with evidence.

Try this Monday

Give students one claim and three sources (one weak); they rank credibility and justify in two sentences each.

Look-fors

  • Students question where information comes from.
  • They check accuracy, validity, bias and origin — not just the first result.
  • They curate, not just collect.
1.4
ISTE 1.4

Innovative Designer

Students use a design process to solve real problems.

What it means

Students use a variety of technologies within a design process to identify and solve problems by creating new, useful or imaginative solutions. They follow a deliberate design process, manage design constraints and calculated risks, develop and refine prototypes in cycles, and persevere with open-ended problems.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Run a design-thinking cycle on a real user need.
  • Prototype with CAD, electronics, or app/UX mockups.
  • Test prototypes with real users and iterate.
  • Document design decisions and trade-offs.

Try this Monday

Give a 20-minute "paper prototype" challenge for a problem in your subject, then a 2-minute peer test round.

Look-fors

  • Students iterate based on testing, not guessing.
  • They tolerate ambiguity and keep going.
  • They can justify design choices.
1.5
ISTE 1.5

Computational Thinker

Students break down problems and use data and algorithms.

What it means

Students develop and employ strategies for understanding and solving problems in ways that leverage technological methods to develop and test solutions — formulating problems, working with data, decomposing, abstracting, modelling, and using algorithmic thinking and automation. In a secondary school this belongs in every subject, not only Computer Science (our emphasis).

Secondary classroom examples

  • Decompose a messy problem into solvable parts.
  • Analyse and visualise data in spreadsheets or code.
  • Build a model or simulation (geography, science, economics).
  • Automate a repetitive task with a simple script or formula.

Try this Monday

Take a real dataset relevant to your topic and have students find one pattern and one question it raises.

Look-fors

  • Students break problems into parts before solving.
  • They use data to support claims.
  • They spot patterns and generalise.
1.6
ISTE 1.6

Creative Communicator

Students choose the right tools to communicate clearly and creatively.

What it means

Students communicate clearly and express themselves creatively for a variety of purposes, choosing the platforms, tools, styles, formats and media appropriate to their goals and audience. They create original work or responsibly repurpose and remix — crediting sources — and use visualisations to communicate complex ideas.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Choose the best medium for the message (infographic, podcast, video, site).
  • Turn data into a clear visualisation.
  • Publish work for an authentic audience, not just the teacher.
  • Remix responsibly with attribution.
  • Remix responsibly and credit sources (Creative Commons, licensing).

Try this Monday

Ask students to explain one concept three ways (a sentence, a diagram, a 30-second audio clip) and pick the clearest.

Look-fors

  • Students match medium to purpose and audience.
  • Their visuals clarify rather than decorate.
  • They publish and respond to feedback.
1.7
ISTE 1.7

Global Collaborator

Students collaborate and broaden perspectives with digital tools.

What it means

Students use digital tools to broaden their perspectives and enrich their learning by collaborating with others and working effectively in teams, locally and globally. They connect with diverse peers and experts, weigh multiple viewpoints, take on team roles, and investigate local and global issues.

Secondary classroom examples

  • Co-create in shared docs with clear team roles.
  • Connect with another class, school or expert.
  • Tackle a global issue (e.g. an SDG) with a real audience.
  • Use collaborative tools to give and act on peer feedback.

Try this Monday

Set a shared document task with named roles (researcher, editor, designer) and a 10-minute handover at the end.

Look-fors

  • Students contribute fairly and use roles.
  • They seek perspectives beyond the classroom.
  • They turn collaboration into a shared product.
